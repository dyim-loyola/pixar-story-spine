Character

Rule 1

Admire trying, not just success

You admire a character more for trying than for their successes. Effort and failure are more compelling than achievement — audiences root for struggle, not victory.

Rule 6

Throw the opposite at them

Figure out what your character is good at or comfortable with, then throw the opposite at them. Constraint reveals character far better than comfort does.

Rule 13

Give characters opinions

Passive, malleable characters might seem likable to write, but they are poison to an audience. Characters must want something and take positions — even wrong ones.

Rule 15

Ask how you would feel

If you were your character in this situation, how would you feel? Honesty grounded in real emotion makes even unbelievable situations feel credible.

Rule 21

Identify with your characters

You can't just write "cool" — ask what would make you actually behave that way. If you can't answer it, the audience won't buy it.

Process

Rule 3

Theme reveals itself at the end

Aim for theme, but you usually only see what the story is really about once you reach the end. Write it, then rewrite with that clarity.

Rule 7

Work out your ending first

Come up with your ending before you figure out the middle. Endings are hard — get yours working early so everything you write points somewhere real.

Rule 8

Finish it and move on

Finish your story and let it go, even if it is not perfect. Done and imperfect beats endlessly revised and unfinished.

Rule 9

List what wouldn't happen next

When you are stuck, make a list of what wouldn't happen next. The interesting path is often hiding inside the discarded pile.

Rule 11

Put the idea on paper

If it stays in your head it is unshareable and perfect and useless. The moment it is written, it becomes real enough to improve.

Rule 12

Discard your first five ideas

Get the obvious material out so you can surprise yourself. The interesting work starts after the predictable ideas are spent.

Rule 17

No work is wasted

If something isn't working, let it go — but its useful parts can show up elsewhere.

Craft

Rule 4

Use the story spine

"Once upon a time ___. Every day ___. One day ___. Because of that ___. Because of that ___. Until finally ___." This enforces causal thinking and gives any story a spine.

Rule 5

Simplify and combine

Simplify and focus. Combine characters. Skip detours. Complexity that doesn't serve the story is dead weight.

Rule 16

Make the stakes clear

Give the audience reasons to root for the character. Show what happens if they fail. Keep raising the bar.

Rule 18

Know your core deeply

"Just doing cool stuff" isn't enough. Cool without meaning deflates quickly once the novelty fades.

Rule 19

Coincidence in is fine, out is cheating

Coincidences that get characters into trouble are great. Getting them out that way is cheating — they must earn their way out.

Rule 22

Find the most economical telling

What is the essence of your story? Once you know that, you can build outward. Everything should be justifiable from that core.

Audience & inspiration

Rule 2

Think like an audience, not a writer

Keep in mind what is interesting to you as an audience, not just what is fun to make. The writer's pleasure and the viewer's pleasure are not always the same.

Rule 10

Pull apart stories you love

Identify what you like in stories that move you. That is a part of you — and you can use it. Studying what works is not copying; it is learning your own taste.

Rule 14

Know why you must tell this story

The belief burning inside you that it feeds on is its heart. If you cannot answer this, the audience will feel the absence even if they cannot name it.

Rule 20

Rearrange a film you dislike

Take apart a movie you don't like and rearrange its pieces. Bad films often have good ingredients in the wrong order.
