2-Minute Product Demo Templates

Three narrative frameworks for structuring a short product walkthrough. Each gives you a different starting point — choose the one that best matches your app, your audience, and how you naturally tell a story. These templates prepare you for the fuller 5–7 minute lifecycle presentation.

Problem-Solution Framework

A direct, product-first structure that moves from user context to features to outcome. It works well when your audience is unfamiliar with the problem space and needs to understand who the user is before they can appreciate what the app does.

This approach is common in product management and startup pitching, where clarity and efficiency matter more than narrative arc. It is the most transferable framework across different audiences and presentation contexts.

Origin This structure draws from standard product management pitch conventions, closely related to the value proposition canvas developed by Alexander Osterwalder. Learn more at strategyzer.com.
Opening
Today I want to introduce [Product Name].
User & Problem
Our primary user is [target persona], someone who [brief user context]. Their biggest challenge is [main pain point]. Right now they have to [current frustrating process], which leads to [negative outcome].
Value Proposition
That is where [Product Name] comes in. Our unique value proposition is: [one sentence describing how the app solves the problem better or faster].
Features
When users open the app, they first [first key interaction], which helps them [benefit]. Next, they can use [Feature 1] to [benefit]. They can also use [Feature 2] to [benefit]. Finally, [Feature 3] brings everything together in [dashboard, summary page, etc.].
Outcome
Before using the app, users struggled with [old experience]. After, they can [improved experience]. Ultimately, [Product Name] helps [target persona] achieve [main outcome].

Delivery tips

  • Open with confidence — saying the product name clearly sets the tone for everything that follows.
  • Keep the user and problem section to two or three sentences maximum. This is not the main event.
  • The features section is where most presenters rush. Slow down and name the benefit after each feature, not just the feature itself.
  • End on the outcome, not on a feature. The last thing the audience hears should be about the user, not the app.

Pixar Story Spine Adapted

A narrative framework that opens with the user's world before introducing the product. By grounding the audience in who the user is and what their daily struggle looks like, it creates emotional investment that makes the product reveal feel earned rather than announced.

This is an adaptation of the Story Spine structure used by Pixar story artists, applied here to a product context. The six stages map directly to the fuller framework you will use in your final lifecycle presentation, making this a useful rehearsal for that longer format.

Learn more about the full Story Spine framework →
Origin The Story Spine was developed by improv teacher Kenn Adams and popularised in storytelling circles by Pixar story artist Emma Coats. Learn more at aerogrammestudio.com.
Stage 1 — Once upon a time
Once upon a time, there was [target persona], someone who [brief description of lifestyle, goals, or situation].
↓
Stage 2 — Every day
Every day, they struggled with [main pain point]. They often had to [current frustrating process], which led to [negative outcome].
↓
Stage 3 — Until one day
Then one day, they found [Product Name]. The unique value proposition is: [one sentence explaining how the app solves the problem better or faster than alternatives].
↓
Stage 4 — Because of that
When they opened the app, they first [first key interaction], which gave them [immediate benefit]. They could use [Feature 1] to [benefit], and [Feature 2] to [benefit].
↓
Stage 5 — Because of that
Finally, [Feature 3] brought everything together in [dashboard, itinerary, summary page, etc.], which meant they no longer had to [old frustrating process].
↓
Stage 6 — Until finally
[Product Name] helped [target persona] achieve [main transformation or outcome]. What used to take [old experience] now became [better experience].

Delivery tips

  • The stage labels are for preparation only — do not read them aloud. The transitions should feel natural, not structured.
  • Stages 1 and 2 set up the emotional stakes. Do not rush them to get to the product — that tension is what makes Stage 3 land.
  • Stay in third person throughout. You are telling the user's story, not pitching your product.
  • Notice how this structure maps to the full Story Spine framework. In your final presentation, each of these stages will expand into a longer section.

AIDA Framework

A persuasion-first structure drawn from marketing and advertising. It opens with a hook designed to capture attention before establishing the user context, then moves to the product and features, and closes with a clear call to action or outcome statement.

AIDA works best when your audience may not already care about the problem you are solving. The Attention stage earns you the right to be heard before you say anything about your app. It is the most audience-aware of the three frameworks.

Origin AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action — was first formulated by advertising pioneer Elias St. Elmo Lewis in the late 1800s and remains one of the most widely used frameworks in marketing communications. Learn more at wikipedia.org.
Attention Start with a hook — a surprising fact, a question, or a pain point stated as a feeling.
[Strong opening statement, surprising fact, question, or emotionally framed pain point.] Example: "Planning a trip should be exciting, but for many people it becomes stressful because there are too many destinations, websites, and booking decisions."
Interest
Our primary user is [target persona], someone who [brief user context]. Their biggest challenge is [main pain point]. Right now they often have to [current frustrating process], which leads to [negative outcome].
Desire
That is why we created [Product Name]. Our unique value proposition is: [one sentence describing how the app solves the problem better or faster].

When users open the app, they first [first key interaction]. Next, they can use [Feature 1] to [benefit]. They can also use [Feature 2] to [benefit]. Finally, [Feature 3] brings everything together in [dashboard, summary page, itinerary, etc.].
Action
Before using the app, users struggled with [old experience]. After using the app, they can [improved experience]. Ultimately, [Product Name] helps [target persona] achieve [main outcome].

Delivery tips

  • The Attention hook is everything. If it lands flat, the rest of the presentation fights an uphill battle. Practise it more than any other line.
  • Name the emotion in the hook, not just the problem. "Stressful" lands better than "inefficient."
  • The Desire section is doing the most work. Give each feature a clear benefit — not just what it does but why it matters to the user.
  • End the Action section on the user's transformation, not on a product feature. Leave the audience thinking about the person, not the app.
