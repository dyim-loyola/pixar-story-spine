Character

Rule 1

Admire trying, not just success

You admire a character more for trying than for their successes. Effort and failure are more compelling than achievement alone — audiences root for struggle, not victory.

Rule 6

Throw the opposite at them

Figure out what your character is good at or comfortable with, then throw the opposite at them and see how they cope. Constraint reveals character far better than comfort does.

Rule 13

Give characters opinions

Passive, malleable characters might seem likable to write, but they are poison to an audience. Characters must want something and take positions — even wrong ones.

Rule 15

Ask how you would feel

If you were your character in this situation, how would you feel? Honesty grounded in real emotion makes even unbelievable situations feel credible to an audience.

Rule 21

Identify with your characters

You must identify with your characters and situations. You can't just write "cool" — ask what would make you actually behave that way. If you can't answer it, the audience won't buy it.

Process

Rule 3

Theme reveals itself at the end

Aim for theme, but you usually only see what the story is really about once you reach the end. Write it, then rewrite with that clarity. Trying to theme-first often produces something airless.

Rule 7

Work out your ending first

Come up with your ending before you figure out the middle. Endings are hard — get yours working early so everything you write points somewhere real.

Rule 8

Finish it and move on

Finish your story and let it go, even if it is not perfect. Move on and do better next time. Done and imperfect beats endlessly revised and unfinished.

Rule 9

List what wouldn't happen next

When you are stuck, make a list of what wouldn't happen next. Often the interesting path is hiding inside the discarded pile — the obvious choices block it.

Rule 11

Put the idea on paper

Putting an idea on paper lets you start fixing it. If it stays in your head, it is unshareable and perfect and useless. The moment it is written, it becomes real enough to improve.

Rule 12

Discard your first five ideas

Discount your first idea, and the second, third, fourth, and fifth. Get the obvious material out so you can surprise yourself. The interesting work starts after the predictable ideas are spent.

Rule 17

No work is wasted

If something isn't working, let it go — but its useful parts can show up elsewhere. Work that doesn't fit the current story is often exactly right for a future one.

Craft

Rule 4

Use the story spine

"Once upon a time there was ___. Every day ___. One day ___. Because of that ___. Because of that ___. Until finally ___." This structure enforces causal thinking and gives any story a spine before you add flesh.

Rule 5

Simplify and combine

Simplify and focus. Combine characters. Skip detours, even when it feels like you are losing good material. Complexity that doesn't serve the story is dead weight.

Rule 16

Make the stakes clear

Give the audience reasons to root for the character. Show what happens if they fail. Keep raising the bar — stakes that plateau make stories feel safe, and safe stories lose people.

Rule 18

Know your core deeply

You have to know your story's core and your characters deeply. "Just doing cool stuff" isn't enough. Cool without meaning deflates quickly once the novelty fades.

Rule 19

Coincidence in is fine, out is cheating

Coincidences that get characters into trouble are great storytelling. Coincidences that get them out of trouble are cheating. The character must earn their way out.

Rule 22

Find the most economical telling

What is the essence of your story — the most economical way to tell it? Once you know that, you can build outward. Everything in the story should be justifiable from that core.

Audience & inspiration

Rule 2

Think like an audience, not a writer

Keep in mind what is interesting to you as an audience, not just what is fun to make or write. The writer's pleasure and the viewer's pleasure are not always the same thing.

Rule 10

Pull apart stories you love

Identify what you like in the stories that move you. That is a part of you — and you can use it. Studying what works is not copying; it is learning your own taste.

Rule 14

Know why you must tell this story

Ask why you must tell this story. The belief burning inside you that it feeds on is its heart. If you cannot answer this, the audience will feel the absence even if they cannot name it.

Rule 20

Rearrange a film you dislike

Take apart a movie you don't like and rearrange its pieces into something you would like. The exercise builds structural instinct — bad films often have good ingredients in the wrong order.
