Pixar Storytelling

Pixar's approach to storytelling is one of the most studied narrative frameworks in the world — not because it comes from animation, but because it mirrors how people actually experience change. This toolkit introduces the core frameworks, explores the Story Spine structure in depth, and provides templates for applying these ideas to your own product presentations.

Overview

What is Pixar storytelling?

Pixar story artist Emma Coats distilled the studio's internal storytelling philosophy into 22 rules — practical principles that guide how every Pixar film is structured, from character design to plot resolution. At the heart of those rules is a single insight: good stories are built on cause and consequence, not on a sequence of events.

Two frameworks sit at the centre of this philosophy — the Story Spine, which provides a six-stage causal structure, and the 22 Rules, which offer guidance on character, craft, process, and audience. Together they form a teachable, repeatable approach to narrative that transfers far beyond filmmaking.

Why it works in the classroom

The Story Spine replaces vague advice — "have a beginning, middle, and end" — with a repeatable method. It does not limit creativity; it channels it by enforcing causal thinking at every stage.

Why it works for product pitches

A product that solves a real problem has a story built into it. The Story Spine gives you the structure to surface that story — moving from user context to disruption to resolution in a way that audiences follow naturally.

The 22 Rules

Beyond structure, the 22 Rules address the harder questions: how to write characters audiences care about, when to abandon material that isn't working, and what separates a story worth telling from one that isn't.

From 2 minutes to 7 minutes

The frameworks scale. The 2-minute demo exercise uses the same causal logic as the full lifecycle presentation — the difference is scope, not structure. Mastering one prepares you for the other.

The Story Spine

A structured approach to narrative

The Story Spine was developed by improv teacher Kenn Adams and became central to Pixar's story development process. It provides six sentence starters that, when completed in sequence, produce a causally coherent story. The key word is because — every stage must follow logically from the one before it.

Watch the video below to see the Story Spine explained and applied, then explore the interactive template to understand each stage in depth.

The Story Spine in practice — how Pixar uses a six-stage causal structure to build narrative momentum.

Stage 1 "Once upon a time…"

Establish [who the story is about], [where they are], and [when]. Anchor the audience so everything that follows has context.

Finding Nemo: Marlin is an anxious clownfish living in a sea anemone with his son Nemo, on a quiet coral reef.

Stage 2 "Every day…"

Show [the character's normal routine]. This baseline is what makes the disruption meaningful — without it, the inciting incident has nothing to push against.

WALL-E: Every day, WALL-E compacts trash alone on an abandoned Earth and watches Hello Dolly before sleeping.

Stage 3 "Until one day…"

Something breaks the pattern. [The inciting incident] does not need to be dramatic — it needs to be disruptive enough to force the character to act.

Up: Carl receives an order to vacate his home and move to a retirement community.

Stage 4 "Because of that…"

The character responds — and [their response creates new problems]. This causal link is what most presentations skip. Events must happen because of each other, not just after each other.

Toy Story: Because Buzz becomes Andy's favourite, Woody's jealousy leads to an accident that strands them both.

Stage 5 "Because of that…"

The consequences compound. [A second cause-and-effect stage] raises the stakes further. Longer stories may need several of these; shorter ones may need only one.

The Incredibles: Because Bob secretly returns to superhero work, his deception pulls the whole family into a far larger threat.

Stage 6 "Until finally…"

A new equilibrium is established — not the same as before, but one that feels earned. [The resolution works because the chain of cause and consequence made it inevitable.]

Inside Out: Riley tells her parents how she truly feels — arriving not at happiness, but at a richer emotional world.

Character

Rule 1

Admire trying, not just success

You admire a character more for trying than for their successes. Effort and failure are more compelling than achievement alone — audiences root for struggle, not victory.

Rule 6

Throw the opposite at them

Figure out what your character is good at or comfortable with, then throw the opposite at them and see how they cope. Constraint reveals character far better than comfort does.

Rule 13

Give characters opinions

Passive, malleable characters might seem likable to write, but they are poison to an audience. Characters must want something and take positions — even wrong ones.

Rule 15

Ask how you would feel

If you were your character in this situation, how would you feel? Honesty grounded in real emotion makes even unbelievable situations feel credible to an audience.

Rule 21

Identify with your characters

You must identify with your characters and situations. You can't just write "cool" — ask what would make you actually behave that way. If you can't answer it, the audience won't buy it.

Process

Rule 3

Theme reveals itself at the end

Aim for theme, but you usually only see what the story is really about once you reach the end. Write it, then rewrite with that clarity. Trying to theme-first often produces something airless.

Rule 7

Work out your ending first

Come up with your ending before you figure out the middle. Endings are hard — get yours working early so everything you write points somewhere real.

Rule 8

Finish it and move on

Finish your story and let it go, even if it is not perfect. Move on and do better next time. Done and imperfect beats endlessly revised and unfinished.

Rule 9

List what wouldn't happen next

When you are stuck, make a list of what wouldn't happen next. Often the interesting path is hiding inside the discarded pile — the obvious choices block it.

Rule 11

Put the idea on paper

Putting an idea on paper lets you start fixing it. If it stays in your head, it is unshareable and perfect and useless. The moment it is written, it becomes real enough to improve.

Rule 12

Discard your first five ideas

Discount your first idea, and the second, third, fourth, and fifth. Get the obvious material out so you can surprise yourself. The interesting work starts after the predictable ideas are spent.

Rule 17

No work is wasted

If something isn't working, let it go — but its useful parts can show up elsewhere. Work that doesn't fit the current story is often exactly right for a future one.

Craft

Rule 4

Use the story spine

"Once upon a time there was ___. Every day ___. One day ___. Because of that ___. Because of that ___. Until finally ___." This structure enforces causal thinking and gives any story a spine before you add flesh.

Rule 5

Simplify and combine

Simplify and focus. Combine characters. Skip detours, even when it feels like you are losing good material. Complexity that doesn't serve the story is dead weight.

Rule 16

Make the stakes clear

Give the audience reasons to root for the character. Show what happens if they fail. Keep raising the bar — stakes that plateau make stories feel safe, and safe stories lose people.

Rule 18

Know your core deeply

You have to know your story's core and your characters deeply. "Just doing cool stuff" isn't enough. Cool without meaning deflates quickly once the novelty fades.

Rule 19

Coincidence in is fine, out is cheating

Coincidences that get characters into trouble are great storytelling. Coincidences that get them out of trouble are cheating. The character must earn their way out.

Rule 22

Find the most economical telling

What is the essence of your story — the most economical way to tell it? Once you know that, you can build outward. Everything in the story should be justifiable from that core.

Audience & inspiration

Rule 2

Think like an audience, not a writer

Keep in mind what is interesting to you as an audience, not just what is fun to make or write. The writer's pleasure and the viewer's pleasure are not always the same thing.

Rule 10

Pull apart stories you love

Identify what you like in the stories that move you. That is a part of you — and you can use it. Studying what works is not copying; it is learning your own taste.

Rule 14

Know why you must tell this story

Ask why you must tell this story. The belief burning inside you that it feeds on is its heart. If you cannot answer this, the audience will feel the absence even if they cannot name it.

Rule 20

Rearrange a film you dislike

Take apart a movie you don't like and rearrange its pieces into something you would like. The exercise builds structural instinct — bad films often have good ingredients in the wrong order.
