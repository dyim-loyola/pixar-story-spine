Pixar Storytelling

Pixar's storytelling philosophy — distilled by story artist Emma Coats — offers a teachable, repeatable approach to narrative that transfers far beyond filmmaking. Use this toolkit to understand the frameworks, then apply them to your own product presentation.

Emma Coats distilled Pixar's internal storytelling philosophy into 22 rules — practical principles that guide how every Pixar film is structured. At the heart of those rules is one insight: good stories are built on cause and consequence, not a sequence of events.

Why it works in the classroom

The Story Spine replaces vague advice — "have a beginning, middle, and end" — with a repeatable method that enforces causal thinking at every stage.

Why it works for product pitches

A product that solves a real problem has a story built into it. The Story Spine surfaces that story — moving from user context to disruption to resolution naturally.

The 22 Rules

Beyond structure, the 22 Rules address the harder questions: how to write characters audiences care about, when to abandon material, and what makes a story worth telling.

From 2 minutes to 7 minutes

The frameworks scale. The 2-minute demo uses the same causal logic as the full lifecycle presentation — the difference is scope, not structure.

Six sentence starters, completed in sequence, produce a causally coherent story. The key word is because — every stage must follow logically from the one before it. Developed by improv teacher Kenn Adams and adopted by Pixar as a story development tool.

Stage 1"Once upon a time…"

Establish [who], [where], and [when]. Anchor the audience before anything is disrupted.

Finding Nemo: Marlin is an anxious clownfish living in a sea anemone with his son Nemo on a quiet coral reef.

Stage 2"Every day…"

Show [the character's normal routine]. This baseline makes the disruption meaningful.

WALL-E: Every day, WALL-E compacts trash alone on an abandoned Earth and watches Hello Dolly before sleeping.

Stage 3"Until one day…"

[The inciting incident] breaks the pattern. It doesn't need to be dramatic — it needs to be disruptive enough to force action.

Up: Carl receives an order to vacate his home and move to a retirement community.

Stage 4"Because of that…"

The character responds — and [their response creates new problems]. Events must happen because of each other, not just after each other.

Toy Story: Because Buzz becomes Andy's favourite, Woody's jealousy leads to an accident stranding them both.

Stage 5"Because of that…"

The consequences compound. [A second cause-and-effect stage] raises the stakes further.

The Incredibles: Because Bob secretly returns to superhero work, his deception pulls the whole family into a far larger threat.

Stage 6"Until finally…"

A new equilibrium is established — not the same as before, but earned. [The resolution feels inevitable because of the chain that preceded it.]

Inside Out: Riley tells her parents how she truly feels — arriving not at happiness, but at a richer emotional world.
